Consulting Case Brief

Which market should we enter next?

ClientD.N.V. Overseas IndustryInternational Export / Commodities Markets evaluatedRussia · Mongolia · Middle East ToolMarket Expansion Command Center
01 — Situation

A growing export business needed to make a focused bet.

D.N.V. Overseas is an India-based export business operating across international commodity and supply markets. By late 2024, the business had established active supply contracts and buyer relationships in Russia and Mongolia, generating $450K+ in contract value across 25+ agreements. Momentum was real but capital and BD capacity were limited.

Management was facing a classic scaling decision: defend and deepen existing markets, or open a third geography. The Middle East — specifically the UAE and GCC corridor — had surfaced as an inbound opportunity, with early buyer interest from trading companies and commodity distributors. The question was whether to pursue it seriously and where it ranked relative to doubling down on Russia and Mongolia.

02 — Problem

Three expansion paths. One BD resource. No framework to choose.

With a single BD lead managing outreach, the business could only build meaningful pipeline in one new market at a time. A wrong call meant 6–9 months of sunk cost: agent fees, outreach effort, compliance groundwork, and opportunity cost on existing markets.

The decision lacked structure. Market enthusiasm from inbound contacts is not the same as market attractiveness. The team needed a way to compare the three options on the same dimensions — demand size, entry cost, competitive pressure, risk, and conversion probability — and translate that into a prioritised recommendation with clear 90-day actions.

03 — Approach

A five-dimension weighted scoring model with scenario sensitivity.

Built the Market Expansion Command Center to structure the decision across five dimensions, each weighted by its sensitivity to capital constraints at this stage of the business:

TAM & demand
20%
Pipeline conversion
25%
Entry cost
25%
Competitive intensity
15%
Operating risk
15%

Each market was scored 1–10 per dimension, weighted, and aggregated. Scenarios were run for conservative, base, and optimistic conversion assumptions. Pipeline contribution and net entry NPV were modelled for a 12-month horizon.

04 — Findings

Middle East won on entry efficiency. Russia held on scale.

Middle East (UAE / GCC) ★

Highest pipeline conversion, lowest entry cost

Existing buyer inbound interest cut cold outreach cost by ~60%. Shared logistics routes from India reduced landed cost. USD-denominated contracts eliminated FX risk. Weighted score: 7.9 / 10.

Russia

Strong on scale, constrained on risk

Largest TAM of the three markets, with established buyer relationships. However, geopolitical risk and payment complexity penalised the operating risk dimension. Weighted score: 6.4 / 10.

Mongolia

Low competition but limited TAM ceiling

Good conversion rate due to thin competition, but market size constrained upside. Best suited as a margin-defence market rather than a growth bet. Weighted score: 5.8 / 10.

05 — Recommendation

Prioritise the Middle East. Set a 90-day pipeline target. Defer Mongolia expansion.

Primary action: Allocate BD capacity to the UAE/GCC corridor for the next 90 days. Target 8 qualified buyer conversations with commodity distributors and trading companies. Objective: 2 signed supply agreements by month 4.

Secondary action: Maintain Russia relationships at current run rate — renewals only, no new market development investment until geopolitical risk score improves.

Defer: Mongolia new-market investment. Serve existing accounts but do not open new outreach campaigns until Middle East pipeline is established.

Decision trigger: If Middle East pipeline conversion rate falls below 15% at 60-day review, reassess weighting and evaluate a pivot back to Mongolia or a hybrid approach.

06 — Outcome

Middle East validated as priority market. Pipeline established within 8 months.

$450K+ total contract value across all three markets
3 active markets — Russia, Mongolia, Middle East
25+ supply contracts closed, pipeline built from zero

The framework correctly identified the Middle East as the highest-efficiency expansion path. The structured scoring approach also gave the team a shared language for evaluating future expansion decisions — moving from intuition-driven to evidence-driven market entry.
